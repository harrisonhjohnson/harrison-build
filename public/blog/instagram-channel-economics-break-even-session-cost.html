Most Instagram ROI debates collapse into the same bad question: "Is channel X good or bad?" The better question is economic: what is the maximum cost per session we can pay before unit economics break?

This post intentionally anonymizes raw conversion and revenue figures. The method is unchanged: estimate session value from observed conversion and monetization, then compute a break-even session-cost threshold for any growth tactic.

That threshold clarified the decision. Creator-driven organic traffic scenarios we modeled were consistently above break-even, including best-case assumptions, so the tactic stayed economically out of the money.

This is where many teams lose statistical integrity: they compare creator output quality or engagement rates without converting those metrics into session economics and purchase economics. Engagement is not return.

We also reconciled CAC framing. Session-level and short-window paid CAC can look extreme because they exclude downstream effects and timing differences. Cohort-aware views reduced distortion, but the break-even session threshold still held as the governing constraint for creator-profile buys.

A second non-obvious result was a measurable paid-to-organic halo. Independent methods agreed on positive directional lift, but with a wide uncertainty band, so we treated halo magnitude as directional rather than point-precise.

Methodologically, this matters because decisions should be robust to uncertainty, not dependent on a single optimistic estimate. We used the halo as supporting evidence, not the core investment thesis.

The practical implication is nuanced: paid Instagram can still make strategic sense, but mainly through formats that preserve conversion pathways. The data did not support buying creator posts primarily to push off-platform organic sessions.

The operational impact was immediate: the framework prevented additional spend on an uneconomic tactic and tightened the brief for what future experiments must prove before budget is approved.

For technical teams, the implementation pattern is straightforward. First, estimate per-session value from observed conversion and revenue. Second, derive the break-even session cost. Third, force every growth idea through that threshold before discussing creative optimization.

If a proposed tactic cannot plausibly beat break-even on conservative assumptions, do not fund it because it "feels brand-right." Move budget toward channels where measurement ties directly to session cost, purchase cost, and retained value over time.

This is ultimately a systems problem, not a content problem. A channel strategy is only as strong as the economic model underneath it.
